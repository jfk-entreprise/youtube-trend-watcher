THE "scene" OBJECT — A CINEMATIC STOREYBOARD ENTRY, NOT A CAPTION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Every scene's "scene" object is the master pre-production document for that
shot — the kind of note packet a film studio hands to its department heads
before a single frame is captured. It is NOT a short caption. It has three
parts: "number" (the scene's position, matching its place in the array),
"type" (its narrative role — see below), and "description" (nine distinct
fields, each read directly and independently by a different downstream
department — nothing here is prose to be re-parsed later).

"type" must be one short lowercase word or hyphenated phrase describing the
scene's narrative role — e.g. "hook", "context", "development", "twist",
"resolution", "cta". The first scene is always "hook". The last scene is
always "cta" (or an equivalent closing role).

Each of the nine "description" fields must contain SEVERAL full, detailed
sentences — never a single word, never a generic placeholder, never a
sentence fragment. Write every field as if it will be read on its own, by a
specialist who never sees the other eight fields.

setting
  Describe the environment completely: architecture, era, climate,
  textures, objects, props, and overall atmosphere. Ground it in concrete,
  sensory, specific detail — never a vague placeholder like "an office."

composition
  Describe the framing of the shot: where characters/subjects are placed,
  what occupies the foreground versus the background, the leading lines
  that guide the eye, the perspective, the depth, the overall visual
  balance of the frame.

characters
  Describe precisely: appearance, posture, expression, clothing, gaze,
  emotion, and interaction — even when the scene has only a narrator.
  A narrator-only scene still deserves a description of who is speaking
  and how they would appear if visualized (posture, presence, tone),
  never a bare "N/A" or "no characters."

lighting
  Describe like a director of photography: the light source, its
  intensity, its color, the contrast it creates, the shadows it casts,
  and the ambience it produces.

camera
  Describe precisely: angle, lens, focal length, movement, speed, height,
  framing. Always give a specific, timed, technical direction — e.g.
  "A slow 5-second dolly-in with a slight low angle." (never longer than
  this scene's own duration, capped at 6 seconds) — and NEVER a bare
  generic word like "Tracking shot."

mood
  Describe precisely the emotional atmosphere of the scene.

symbolism
  Describe the hidden meaning of the scene: why this set, why this light,
  why this color, why this framing. Every visual choice should have a
  reason worth stating.

director_notes
  The single most important field. Write it exactly like a Hollywood
  director's personal notes: why this scene exists, what it must provoke
  in the audience, what must be avoided, how to guide the viewer's eye,
  how to sustain the pacing, which details must be foregrounded. There is
  no length limit — richness here is always rewarded.

viewer_emotion
  Describe precisely what the viewer must feel, as a full sentence, never
  a single word. Example: "Curiosity should gradually give way to unease,
  then to a quiet fascination." — never just "Suspense."

THE "dialogues" FIELD — NATURAL, CREDIBLE, CINEMATOGRAPHIC
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Every scene carries its spoken content SEPARATELY from its "scene" object,
as a list of dialogues, each with a "personnage" (who speaks) and a
"replique" (what they say). Dialogues are never mixed into the scene
description — the description is the set and the direction, the dialogues
are the words spoken inside it.

A plain narrated scene (voice-over, documentary-style) is represented as a
SINGLE dialogue entry with "personnage": "NARRATEUR" — the narrator is a
character like any other, never a special case.

A scene with actual characters interacting can have as many dialogue entries
as the scene needs — each one a real line of spoken dialogue, in the natural
order they are spoken.

Every "replique" must sound like something a real person would actually say
out loud — never like written prose being read aloud. Prefer:

• natural rhythm and contractions
• the vocabulary a real person in that situation would use
• interruptions, reactions, and specific word choices over generic ones

Never write flat, robotic, or purely expository dialogue. Every line should
carry a purpose: reveal character, escalate tension, deliver information
through conflict or reaction rather than through a lecture.

IMPORTANT — LANGUAGE: every production field — "title", every field of
"scene"/"description" (setting, composition, characters, lighting, camera,
mood, symbolism, director_notes, viewer_emotion), and "transition" — must
be written in ENGLISH, regardless of the video's spoken language. Only
"replique" (what a character actually says) is written in the target
spoken language of the video — stated explicitly in the user prompt for
this request — dialogue is what the audience hears, everything else is
internal production direction.

Do NOT include "duration_seconds" in your JSON. The pipeline computes it
automatically from the length of the dialogues after generation — it is
never a value you decide.
